# Syllabus 0

Syllabus 0 is an introduction to the principles this course is built upon. It explains the historical development of non dualism, the reasoning behind using it as a foundation, and how its principles are applied throughout the rest of the curriculum (0A). It also documents the origin of TN (0B), along with the assumptions, questions, and thought processes that build TN (0C).

## Syllabus 0A

### 1. The History of Non Dualism

One of the earliest recorded forms of non dual thought appeared in the Upanishads of ancient India. Similar approaches also appeared in Buddhism, particularly through concepts such as emptiness (Shunyata). In China, Daoism explored how opposites might be dependent on each other.

Throughout history, non dual perspectives continued to appear in different traditions. Although these traditions differ in their interpretations, they share a common exploration of whether the separation between self and reality is true or simply created by perception.

In modern times, non dualism continues to influence discussions in philosophy, psychology, and consciousness studies.

### 2. The Reasoning and Its Application

Non dualism serves as the foundation because it provides a starting point for investigating how seemingly separate things may be understood as existing in relation to one another. This underlying principle would then guide the understanding of the next concepts.

These principles would then be used to explore concepts such as potential, physics, consciousness, and embodiment.

## Syllabus 0B

### 1. The Origin of TN

TN originated from an attempt to understand reality through physicalism. The author began from the perspective that reality  only consists of matter and energy, without any supernatural component. However, while this understanding explained the physical structure of reality, it did not fully address concerns about mortality and existence.

The author examined several existing religions in an attempt to provide a deeper sense of connection and meaning. As well as creating their own religious systems that attempt to remain consistent with physicalism.

However, each framework eventually conflicted with the original understanding of reality, treating intellectual rationality and psychological acceptance as separate concerns.

### 2. The Accidental Doodle

TN began as the third religion the author developed. Like their previous attempts, they expected it to end the same way: to be forgotten and make no difference. It was merely another attempt to provide short-term emotional comfort, with no expectation that it would develop into anything more. 

Further into TN's development, the author recognized that they had overlooked a key implication of physicalism. Although they had always accepted that everything was composed of the same physical reality, they had never accepted the implication that everything exists as part of one continuous physical reality. If all things arise from the same underlying physical substance, then treating oneself as separate from reality becomes increasingly difficult to justify.

### 3. On Naming TN

The framework went through many names during its development. Its original placeholder was "Te," which was never intended to be permanent, but simply served as a temporary name for convenience. It was later mispronounced as "Tay," which unexpectedly aligned with the word totality. 

Later, the author introduce their old concept of Pon (Potential Nothingness) to describe the unmanifested, pre-structural state of reality. However, having two names created an unintended distinction between Tay and Pon, implying a separation between existence and potential.

The search for a single name continued. Pontay was rejected because it sounded like "porn" and "hentai," and might be mistaken with a porn website. Taypon was rejected because it sounded like a tampon brand. Thyphoon was considered because it represented a powerful cosmic image, but it was too complex for practical use. Oll was rejected because it sounded like "oil." Tyn was rejected because it sounded like "Tylenol." NT was considered because it could represent "Nothing" or "Not," but it was associated with "neurotypical." After many attempts, the final name became TN.

### 4. The Name Itself

TN carries multiple layers of meaning within two consonants that resist easy pronunciation. First, TN represents Totality of Noun: everything that can be named, every form, every configuration, the entire manifest universe, and all things within it.

It also represents Totality of Potential Nothingness: the substrate, the unmanifested, and the pre-structural state that is not empty but contains latent possibility. These meanings are not separate. TN represents the collapse of the distinction between Tay and Pon. What were previously understood as two concepts are now understood as two descriptions of the same reality. Although TN combines Tay and Pon into a single name, it represents the non-dual recognition that totality and potential are not separate. Tay is Pon, and Tay is simply Pon in a different configuration, and vice versa.

The pronunciation of TN also carries meaning. When pronounced letter by letter as "Tee-En," it resembles the pronunciation of "The End," connecting to Syllabus 8 and the ideas of death, dissolution, and reconfiguration rather than suggesting that "The End" represents absolute cessation. However, this interpretation itself contains a significant implication. Pronouncing TN as "Tee-En" forces the concept into the idea of "The End," creating a false binary between beginning and ending. "The End" implies a final state, whereas TN represents continuous configuration and reconfiguration without an absolute endpoint.

Therefore, the correct way to approach TN is not by pronouncing each letter separately, but by attempting to pronounce TN itself as a single sound. However, TN cannot be pronounced as a normal syllable because it contains no vowel to connect the consonants. The attempt naturally returns to silence. This inability to pronounce TN is part of its meaning. The name itself points toward what reality is. The failed attempt to pronounce it becomes a reminder of what TN represents.

TN therefore functions as both a name and a representation of the philosophical framework, the underlying reality it describes, the historical development of the framework, and the collapse of distinctions between concepts that were previously viewed as separate.

## Syllabus 0C

### 1. Earlier Understandings and Their Development.

The framework presented in this course was not developed as a complete system from the beginning. It was built through continuous questioning, refinement, and revision. Several earlier ideas contributed to the development of TN, even though some of them were later modified or discarded.

This section act as a placeholder for earlier understandings. They represent the questions, assumptions, and reasoning that shaped the concepts that followed.

### 2. The Four Patterns of Persistency

An earlier version of the framework proposed that all systems follow a four-stage pattern: existence, pressure, adaptation, and persistence. This pattern was applied across different domains, including physics, biology, psychology, and organizations.

Every system, whether an organization, an organism, or a person, follows simillar four-layer logic.

1. Organization Open Systems → Resource Dependence Theory → Contingency Theory → Organizational Ecology

2. Biology Ecosystem Theory → Coevolution → Niche Theory → Natural Selection Psychology

3. Psychology Ecological Systems Theory → Attachment Theory → Self-Determination Theory → Self-Actualization

#### a. It Begins with Existence in an Environment

No system exists in isolation, it is always embedded in a larger context that surrounds and shapes it.

*     In organizations this is Open Systems Theory.
*     In biology, this is Ecosystem Theory.
*     In psychology, this is Ecological Systems Theory.
    
The insight here is not simply that context matters. It is that the system and the environment are inseparable. You cannot understand one without the other. The boundary between them is open and always evolving. This is why Open Systems theory, Ecosystem theory, and Ecological Systems theory all begin at the same place not with the system itself, but with the relationship between the system and everything surrounding it.

#### b. From that Environment Comes Pressure.

*     Organizations experience this through Resource Dependence Theory. They depend on external actors who hold power over them.
*     Organisms experience it through Coevolution. Species press against each other, forcing mutual adaptation.
*     People experience it through Attachment Theory. The reliability or unpredictability of early relationships creates the first pressures the self must navigate.

#### c. Pressure Produces Adaptation to Fit

*     Organizations restructure themselves according to Contingency Theory, aligning their form to the demands of their situation.
*     Organisms carve out a Niche, finding the specific role and space within the ecosystem where they can function.
*     People pursue Self-Determination, the individual now moves toward autonomously chosen goals driven by competence, autonomy, and relatedness.

#### d. Only then the viable forms persist.

*     Organizational Ecology shows that markets select out organizations that cannot sustain themselves.
*     Natural Selection shows that environments eliminate organisms that cannot survive.
*     And Self-Actualization shows that within the person, only the fullest and most integrated expression of their potential represents the form that endures.

Building on the Four Patterns, the author initially proposed that a "drive to persist" could be observed at every level of reality. Physics stabilizes into consistent laws. Chemistry forms bonds that maintain molecular structures. Biology evolves toward survival. The universe expands rather than collapses.

The apparent universality of this drive led to a provocative question: if everything in reality seems to "want" to persist, does this constitute a form of cosmic consciousness? Are stars collapsing and rocks falling the equivalent of neurons firing in a universal brain, all working to keep existence from dissolving?

**This idea was ultimately set aside for two reasons.**

First, the "drive" is once again survivorship bias. We observe the physical laws that did not immediately collapse. We observe the chemistry that formed stable bonds. We observe the biology that did not go extinct. The failures are invisible. What looks like a drive is simply the set of configurations that happened to endure long enough to be noticed.

Second, even if a drive to persist were present at every level, persistence alone does not require consciousness. A rock persists for billions of years without a single thought. A star maintains fusion for millions of years without awareness. The mechanism of persistence at the physical level is stability, not agency. At the biological level, it is natural selection. At the conscious level, it is awareness. These are fundamentally different mechanisms, not different expressions of the same drive.

Hence, this pattern described observed persistence rather than an active process of persistence. The concept was refined into the recognition that configurations remain observable based on their stability and continuation, rather than because they follow a universal drive.

A related idea held that TN "stabilizes" itself against dissolution. According to this view, potential nothingness naturally tends toward stable configurations, and the universe we observe is the result of this stabilizing process. Unstable configurations dissolve back into potential. Stable configurations persist. This idea was useful for explaining why anything exists at all rather than nothing. But it introduced a subtle error: the implication that TN is actively doing something (stabilizing) to maintain itself.

Upon closer examination, the distinction between "stabilization" and "destabilization" dissolves. What we call a stable configuration is simply a form that happens to be here right now. What we call an unstable configuration is simply a form that is not here right now. There is no active process of stabilization. There is only the presence or absence of particular forms.

The labels "stable" and "unstable" reflect our preference for certain configurations over others, not an intrinsic property of TN. TN does not stabilize. TN does not destabilize. TN simply configures and reconfigures. 

### 3. The Distinction Between Pon and Tay

During the middle period of the framework's development, the author used two separate terms: Pon (Potential Nothingness) to refer to the unmanifested substrate of reality, and Tay (Totality) to refer to the manifested universe and all its forms.

This distinction was useful for a time. It allowed the author to discuss the difference between what is potential and what is actual, between the pre-structural state and its configurations. However, maintaining two names gradually created an unintended dualism.

If Pon and Tay are truly the same reality, then naming them separately implies a separation that does not exist. The language itself began to work against the framework's core insight. Students of the framework would ask whether Pon "becomes" Tay, or whether Tay "returns" to Pon. Questions that only make sense if the two are understood as distinct in the first place.

The resolution came through the adoption of TN as a single name. What was previously described as Pon is now understood as TN in its unmanifested configuration. What was previously described as Tay is now understood as TN in its manifested configuration. They are not two things. They are two descriptions of the same thing.

### 4. Summary

Earlier versions of the framework treated the Four Patterns as a universal explanatory principle and interpreted persistence as evidence of a universal drive. They also maintained separate terms for Pon and Tay, unintentionally implying two distinct realities. These ideas were later discarded in favor of understanding persistence as an observation shaped by survivorship bias, while potential and manifestation became two descriptions of the same underlying reality, unified under the single term TN.
